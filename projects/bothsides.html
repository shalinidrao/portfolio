Both Sides: Filter Bubble Startup


Teammates: Nivedita Dhuliya, Napol Rachatasumrit, Thomas Yager
As part of my Digital Service Innovation class, myself and a team of three others developed a startup meant to mitigate media bias via a browser plugin.

Opportunity Framing

Opportunity framing came relatively easily since it was just building off of Napol’s pitch of the original idea. He had built a lot of the necessary analysis models already; it was really only a matter of initial tweaks to make sure we all agreed with the problem space we wanted to attack. We added some new research (four brains is better than one) to really flesh out the analysis models. At this point, we didn’t really decide on an actual implementation of the service, only what it would actually do. It was still quite an abstract idea of the benefit we wanted to give users.

We looked more into the competitors and the PESTLE analysis to come up with a final discussion of what we wanted the problem focus to be.

As for feedback, we got the first of what would be many occurrences of the same criticism that we needed to push the actual harms of the “Filter Bubble” problem is if we wanted to make it our main motivator for why any user should adopt our service. We focused too much on the features in our competing services instead of how it actually plays into the scenario we were focused on. This helped us in our future MVP pitch directions for how to frame our service as a solution to problems in everyday life.

    Our Main Focus:
  • Develop website as well as mobile app
  • Leverage NLP and ML to curate and recommend news
  • Different categories for news
  • A hybrid revenue model

Final Pitch

With our final pivot made, we only had to tweak our value model to show that our value would be targeting both students and institutions, to reflect the new customer-user distinction that came with our revenue model shift. We received positive feedback when presenting this change to the class, with a few questions about how to roll out an MVP in this way to show the school that the service was viable. Luckily, some of our interviews were with CMU officials who would be directly involved in the establishment of such a deal between BothSides and an educational institution. The main requirement for such a partnership would be concrete evidence of actual benefits the service could bring to the student body.

With our main principles locked down, we went into our Pitch Video with a heavier focus on highlighting problems in daily life that act as current barriers to understanding multiple perspectives of the news. We created animations to help depict a more dymamic representation of these problems and cut down all our research into bite-size chunks that would be snappy and stick with our audience.

We went into the investor showcase excited to answer more questions from our guests. We received multiple positive comments about the timeliness of our service as well as several critical questions about the feasibility of rolling out our service on a large scale. We were very pleased to place 4th in both the investor and student categories. We all agreed that should the opportunity come up, we would all be interested in the actual development and deployment of BothSides. We would aim to first get our service to be used in specific civics and poltiical science courses where they would have the most initial obvious value in an academic setting.

We thoroughly enjoyed working on BothSides and learned so much throughout this class and project.
